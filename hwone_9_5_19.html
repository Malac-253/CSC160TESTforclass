We learned in class that computer science is the study of the process or problem-solving.
Already, within the definition, we can see the importance of soft skills, something that the
video touched profoundly on. The video touched on 5 points I found very important to my future
as a computer scientist.
Pargraph 1

Point one was the idea that collaboration happens more than solo work and is more beneficial
to the project than I had believed. I have to keep in mind that, like larger projects, Rome
was not Built-in one day and also not by one person. Collaboration is going to happen with
everyone, not just coders. There will be product managers, designers, and also testers that
I will need to be able to communicate with well. Communication will help the working process.
When it comes to code reviews and systematic examinations, my ability to communicate will improve
the feedback I can get from others.

Pargraph 3

Another point stressed in the video, and the syllabus is the importance of soft skills. Hard work,
determination, communication, problem-solving, are skills needed to excel in today's working environment.
Merely knowing the technical syntax, however necessary, is not enough alone to work in computer science.
The class will make an effort to get us ready for this by having a strict late policy to ensure
punctuality and diligence.

Pargraph 4

The video also stressed the point that there is no "end" in a figurative sense there is always more
work to be done. The class syllabus trains us for this by incentivizing that we do more than
what is expected. By giving an average score of 75% for doing what is expected, students will
have to do more than the average to get an above-average score in the course. Honestly, I like these
criteria, some nights I expect to be rough, but in the long run, it'll be a nice way to make sure
that I am experimenting in the class.
Pargraph 5

Empathy for the people who will interact with your end product is also another point of importance in
the video. Your code is readable, and it has enough context so that anyone can look at it and provide
feedback or help if necessary. It is also important that your end product is made with the user niche
in mine. For example, older people might need larger buttons, or kids might want a more colorful GUI.
It is at this point that I would like to suggest a potential addition to the class syllabus. A low-weighted
project outline would allow students to spend more time in the planning phase too put more thought into the
end product. This way, students will have a chance to consider the end-users better using the product.
Especially if empathy is stressed, then this outline will allow the students to spend a bit more time
making sure that they are keeping their users and helpers in mind.
